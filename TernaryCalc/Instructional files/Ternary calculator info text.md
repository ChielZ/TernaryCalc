This is a calculator built around balanced ternary, a number system that exposes the structure of arithmetic more directly than the decimal notation we use every day. It uses three 'trit' symbols: '\' (the lowest value), '|' (the centre), and '/' (the highest). You can read these as -1, 0, +1, or as down/centre/up, or as left/middle/right — the essential point is that one of them sits at the centre and the other two are mirror images of each other on either side of it.

That mirror symmetry is the whole game. In standard decimal, a negative number is a positive number with a '−' sign tacked on; the negativity lives in an annotation, not in the digits themselves. In balanced ternary, negation IS reflection: '-5' is literally the mirror image of '5', and you can read a number's sign just by looking at its glyphs. Subtraction stops being a separate operation and becomes 'add the mirror' — what mathematics calls adding the additive inverse, made visible.

Numbers are written in place value, just like decimal. A single trit carries a value from -1 to +1, two trits from -4 to +4, three trits from -13 to +13, with each new place multiplying the available range by three. Three trits grouped together — a 'tri-trit' — covers any integer from -13 to +13, and the calculator displays numbers in tri-trit clusters so they can be read as if in base 27, with each tri-trit acting as a single 'digit' running from -13 through 0 to +13. The notation is still pure base-3 underneath; the grouping is a convenience for the eye, the same way commas separate decimal numbers into thousands.

The fractional point works symmetrically to the integer side: each tri-trit to its right is divided by 27, then 729, and so on. The 'ones' tri-trit (the one whose place value is 1) is bracketed by hollow dots whenever the number reaches across both sides, making the symmetric place-value structure visually unmissable. Within each tri-trit, leading zeros are stripped on the integer side and trailing zeros on the fractional side, so the significant digits always cluster toward the centre.

There are five operations. Addition (+) and multiplication (→) work as you'd expect, with the small twist that multiplication has a mirror twin (←), in which '\' (= -1) plays the role of the identity instead of '/' (= +1). There is no subtraction key — 'flip' mirrors a number into its negative, and addition combined with flip behaves as subtraction would. Likewise there is no division key — 'invert' produces the reciprocal, and multiplication combined with invert behaves as division would. The two unary operators each carry a small bar in their glyph that IS the value the operation leaves untouched: a vertical '|' (the trit for zero, the fixed point of flip) for flip, and a horizontal stroke (standing in for one, the fixed point of invert) for invert. Each glyph carries the identity element of its own operation, drawn into it.

What this gets you, beyond curiosity, is a system where the awkwardnesses of decimal arithmetic largely go away. There are no sign rules to remember (negative times negative just falls out of trit multiplication). There is no signed zero. Truncation is unbiased: the maximum error from chopping off digits is always exactly half of one place value, so truncating wherever you like gives you the nearest representable answer for free. Subtraction and division stop being primary operations and become the natural compositions they actually are. The system is more verbose than decimal — you write more glyphs per number — but it is also a more honest representation of the underlying mathematics, surfacing structure that decimal hides under several centuries of accumulated convention.
